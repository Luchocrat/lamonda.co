Social Yaks / Card Game Design
The Brief
01

Give kids from rough upbringings a safe space to talk through it.

Social Yaks is a card game designed as a community outreach tool — specifically for young people from difficult backgrounds who need a structured, safe way to talk about what they've been through. The cards prompt conversations that break the ice and work through the barriers that trauma builds up. Not therapy. A game. The access point matters.

The brief was to give this idea a visual identity that matched the mission: character-driven, warm but not naive, with illustration and custom lettering that made the game feel approachable and real. The animals represent how deep the questions go — and the intention in every expression makes that depth almost palpable.

Process
02
01
Characters as Conversation Starters
Each animal in the deck was designed to carry emotional weight — not just look interesting. The expressions had to communicate something before the card was flipped. Curiosity, vulnerability, warmth, depth. Dozens of iterations per character until the feeling was right. The illustration style had to be accessible to kids while respecting the seriousness of the conversations it would open.
02
Lettering as Brand Voice
Custom lettering was used to brand the game itself — not a typeface, actual hand-crafted type. This gave Social Yaks a voice that felt personal and made. Not like a product. Like something someone cared enough to make by hand. The lettering system extended across card backs, packaging, and the Couples Edition.
03
The Animal Hierarchy
The animal hierarchy wasn't decorative — it was functional. Different animals signal different depths of question. Players can read the energy of a card before they engage with it. This creates a natural flow of escalating vulnerability in the game, making it easier to open up progressively rather than all at once.
04
Couples Edition Expansion
The Couples Edition grew naturally from the original system. A new set of cards tailored specifically to relationship challenges — same illustration language, same lettering DNA, new animal characters tuned to that context. Proof the system had range and a second audience ready to find it.
